Direction

I want to work in the moment before misalignment becomes momentum.

I came to this work as a product and UX designer. I was good at execution. What kept standing out to me was that the real problem was often not the interface — it was what happened before the interface.

A misframed brief. A hallway decision. A silent assumption that hardened into a direction before anyone named the real issue. That is the friction I want to design against.

Argument
AI expands leverage. It does not expand accountability.
The question is not whether to use AI. The question is whether a team has decided what AI cannot be asked to decide.
Point of View

The value of design is
shifting upstream.

As execution gets faster, the value of design moves into framing, prioritization, alignment, and responsible decision-making. What becomes rare is not output. It is the ability to reduce many possible directions to one worth pursuing, and explain why.

What accountability still holds

The interpretation of context. The navigation of competing values. The recognition of trust risk. The final rationale for a decision that affects real people.

How I Decide

A real operating system
does not treat every value equally.

It makes priorities visible. These are the three principles I return to when speed, ambiguity, and pressure begin to distort decision-making, and they stay visible before the cards ever begin.
01

Make trade-offs visible.

Hidden trade-offs do not disappear. They return later as scope creep, stakeholder conflict, or user confusion. My job is to surface them while there is still a choice.

In practice: I will stop a review moving toward a decision and ask, “What are we trading off here?” even when the room wants to move on.
02

Use AI to think harder.

AI is an amplifier. It makes strong thinking stronger, and undisciplined thinking louder. I use it to widen exploration and accelerate synthesis, but not to outsource accountability.

In practice: I do not move forward with an AI-supported direction unless I can explain, in my own words, why it serves the user and what it costs.
03

Trust is a design outcome.

Trust is built through repeated decisions that respect the user’s time, intelligence, and autonomy. It is damaged through repeated decisions that do not.

In practice: I will name the trust cost of a decision even when the metric says to optimize anyway.

How I Collaborate

Misalignment is usually subtle before it becomes expensive.

These are not design reviews. A design review asks: Does this look right? These ask: Are we solving the right problem, and do we agree on what we are trading away?

Framing Check +
What problem are we actually solving?

Before reviewing any output, the team answers this out loud. If there are three different answers in the room, that is the design problem.

When to use it: at the start of any review where direction has not been explicitly aligned.
Trade-off Review +
What are we optimizing for, and what are we giving up?

Every direction involves a trade-off. This ritual makes hidden costs visible before they reappear later as delay, conflict, or user confusion.

When to use it: before any decision that will constrain future options.
Alignment Reset +
Where are product, engineering, and design interpreting the goal differently?

Disagreement is diagnostic data. This ritual surfaces differences in language, assumptions, and success criteria before they fragment the work.

When to use it: when the team is moving fast, but conversations feel slightly off-register.
Decision Log +
What did we decide, why, and what remains uncertain?

A decision log is not documentation. It is institutional memory. That is not paperwork. It is accountability infrastructure.

When to use it: after every decision that will be difficult to reverse.
When tension stalls a decision

I use VPEC-T to clarify what is actually driving the disagreement and shift the conversation from personal preference to shared strategic diagnosis.

Values

What each side is trying to protect.

Policies

What constraints are non-negotiable.

What triggered urgency or escalation.

Content

What information is missing, contested, or overloaded.

Where confidence has already weakened.

I Will Optimize For

User trustIncluding when it slows down shipping.
Long-term valueIncluding when short-term metrics look better without it.
Accessibility and clarityAs non-negotiable, not as stretch goals.
Responsible use of AIWhich means I can explain every decision AI informed.
Systems that reduce confusionNot systems that exploit it.

I Will Not Optimize For

Deceptive engagementEven when A/B data shows it converts better in the short term.
Short-term conversion at the cost of trustEven under deadline pressure.
Automation without human accountabilityEspecially at scale.
Growth that depends on confusion or extractionRegardless of business rationale.
Product decisions that weaken user autonomyEven when the user does not notice.
Red line example
Scenario

If AI recommends a pattern that increases conversion by reducing user clarity, the system treats that as a red-line decision.

Accountability checkpoint

What is being gained? What is being hidden? Who absorbs the trust cost later? If the gain depends on confusion, the direction is rejected.

What I’m Building Toward

Most design systems describe how to make things. This one describes how to make better decisions about what to make.

I am building toward a practice where direction, judgment, and responsibility are designed into how I work, not added later as reflection.

AI is part of that workflow, but never as a way to distribute responsibility away from the people who hold it.
